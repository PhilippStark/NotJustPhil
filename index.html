About

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow and Manager of the Hägerstrand Lab at the Department of Human Geography, Lund University. My work sits at the intersection of computational social science, economic geography, and machine learning — using probabilistic and complexity-oriented methods to understand human behaviour and social systems at scale.

My research background spans two complementary domains. During my doctorate at the University of Tübingen I studied cognition and educational technology through virtual reality and eye tracking. Since joining Lund in 2024 I have extended this to large-scale register data, spatial modelling, and labour market dynamics. Both strands share a commitment to building interpretable, socially actionable models of human systems.

03

Research

My overarching goal is to understand humans through computational, probabilistic, and complexity-oriented science — connecting micro-level cognitive processes with macro-level social dynamics. My work spans two career phases, now increasingly unified into a single computational framework for modelling human-driven systems.

Human Capital & Career Trajectories Current

Using large-scale Swedish register data (LISA, RAMS, CFAR), I develop latent measurement models of human capital. Individual careers are encoded with transformer-based sequence models and graph formulations of labour market transitions to learn low-dimensional embeddings of skills and career states. A first manuscript has been accepted at PAKDD 2026. Ongoing work develops latent state-space models of labour market participation and models human capital mobility as a network-driven mechanism of knowledge diffusion across firms and regions.

Human Mobility, Place & Opportunity Current

This strand examines residential mobility, urban–rural transitions, and regional opportunity structures, combining longitudinal sequence modelling with geospatial analysis and spatial accessibility measures. Current extensions examine entrepreneurial pathways and rural renewal processes, investigating how selective mobility interacts with regional opportunity differentials.

Informal Institutions & AI-Based Media Analysis Current

I develop semantic identification frameworks for informal institutions using large language models applied to policy-relevant text. Projects span EU textile waste governance, green transition discourse in regional newspaper corpora, and AI-powered analysis of advertising in Ethiopian souk shops using vision–language models.

Causal Machine Learning Emerging

An emerging strand in my research agenda is the move from predictive to causal inference in computational social science. Grounded in my probabilistic philosophy of science and critical realism, I am developing structural and graphical causal models to address inequality, sustainable development, and regional resilience. The emphasis is on counterfactual analysis and adversarial vulnerability — identifying conditions under which alternative outcomes could have emerged, isolating underlying causal mechanisms rather than correlational patterns. This work bridges representation learning with causal modelling to produce models that are not only accurate but genuinely explanatory and policy-relevant.

Virtual Reality, Eye Tracking & Cognition PhD Research

My doctoral work established VR as an experimental platform for studying learning and cognition. I developed immersive VR classrooms and multimodal data pipelines showing how presentation modes and interaction design affect attention, mental rotation, memory consolidation, and learning outcomes. A key methodological contribution is the use of eye tracking as a proxy for latent cognitive processes: gaze transition entropy measures, attention network analyses, and interpretable ML pipelines to infer cognitive load and teaching expertise — published in Scientific Reports, Computers in Human Behavior, IEEE VR, ISMAR, and ETRA.

Language, Interaction & Teaching Quality PhD Research

Using large language models and text embeddings to analyse classroom transcripts, I demonstrated how AI can scale qualitative educational research while maintaining interpretability — including GPT-assisted rubric-based scoring and NLP-based interpretability with SHAP values. This work bridges my cognitive science background with my current interests in AI-assisted social science.

Teaching

My teaching is grounded in the belief that university education should empower students to think critically and develop skills they can apply beyond the classroom. I see myself not as an examiner but as a tutor and guide — creating a safe, stimulating environment where questions are welcome and theory connects to practice. This is closely tied to students' motivation and engagement, which I actively facilitate.

Student-Centred Learning

Students are co-producers of knowledge. I use interactive activities, group work, and problem-based exercises so that learning is an active process, not passive reception.

Formative Feedback

Short feedback loops help students monitor their own progress. I integrate weekly feedback forms and iterative reflection into course design, prioritising formative over purely summative assessment.

Reducing Cognitive Barriers

For quantitative content I emphasise clear structure, step-by-step scaffolding, and explicit management of statistics anxiety — so cognitive resources go to learning, not to confusion.

Technology & AI Integration

I use browser-based coding (Google Colab) to lower technical barriers, flipped video inputs to free synchronous time for application, and integrate critical discussions of AI tools into coursework.
